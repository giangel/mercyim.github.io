OUTPATIENT CLINIC

We continued to operate a permanent outpatient clinic in Osi-Ekiti. At this brick-and-mortar clinic, we treat walk-in patients, providing identical services as our Mobile Clinics. The seven-room clinic facility was donated to Mercy International Mission by a local family and is open full-time, five days a week. Mercy continued to operate an office clinic Monday through Friday from 8:00 a.m. until 4:30 p.m. We also operate mobile vans that travel from village to village providing medical care and screening to the people of Osi-Ekiti and its surrounding communities within a 15 km radius. In 2018, Mercy office clinic and mobile unit treated 1283 patients, comprising of 130 children, 786 women, and 385 men. In the past year, the most common ailment treated includes hypertension, malarial fever, diabetes mellitus, upper respiratory tract infection, muscular pain, urinary tract infection, peptic ulcer diseases, osteoarthritis, and gastroenteritis. Hypertension, malaria fever, and osteoarthritis had the highest prevalence in comparison with ailments we treated.

SERVICES AVAILABLE INCLUDE:

  • Basic Health and Vision Screening
  • HIV/AIDS - detection, prevention, and care
  • Malaria - detection, prevention, and care
  • Management & treatment of common illness, such as hypertension, diabetics, typhoid, arthritis, etc.
  • Follow up care at the patient's home
  • Health awareness education and training

Public Health Education/Awareness

People living in poverty usually haven't been educated about when, why, and where to access healthcare. Many wrongly believe that their disease is irreversible and permanent, and they are not aware of medical treatments that can improve or cure their condition. For example, few people in remote villages are aware that many of the forms of blindness prevalent in Nigeria can be cured through surgery. Additionally, general public health education is lacking, such as knowledge of proper hygiene and water treatment. In this way, a lack of education leads to the spread of disease and also prevents patients from seeking medical care. A lack of understanding about the origins of the disease can also lead to sociocultural stigmas, but we believe that the vicious triad of poverty, ignorance, and disease can be reversed by having doctors and nurses available to see patients regularly in a conducive environment. Beyond providing vital medical care, we also place deliberate emphasis on spreading the message of preventive medicine among our target population. In this part of the world, ignorance is a major factor in the spread of diseases. Our staff delivers public health lectures and training at religious and social gatherings to actively combat this trend.
